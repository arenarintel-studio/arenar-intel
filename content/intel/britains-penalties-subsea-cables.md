Britain has announced plans to introduce tougher penalties for damage to subsea internet cables as part of efforts to strengthen protection of critical national infrastructure.

The proposed measures would allow stronger action against shipowners and operators found responsible for reckless damage to undersea communication networks, including potential prison sentences in serious cases.

Subsea cables carry the vast majority of international internet traffic and support financial transactions, communications and digital services across the country.

Telecoms Minister Liz Lloyd said the government intends to update existing legislation and close legal gaps that could be exploited by hostile actors targeting critical infrastructure.

The plans follow growing concerns about threats to undersea cables and other strategic assets. British officials have previously raised concerns about Russian activities near critical infrastructure in and around UK waters.

The government said new measures would also address accidental cable damage caused by fishing activity and vessels dragging anchors, which remain among the most common causes of outages.

Officials are consulting industry stakeholders on the possibility of establishing a British flagged cable repair vessel and reviewing regulations to speed up the deployment of new subsea cables.

The proposed reforms form part of a broader effort to improve the resilience and security of the country's communications infrastructure.
