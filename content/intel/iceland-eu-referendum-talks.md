Iceland’s parliament has voted in favour of holding a referendum on whether to restart European Union accession talks, setting up a nationwide vote scheduled for August 29.

The proposal forms part of a government-backed two-step plan that could eventually lead to EU membership if approved in a second referendum later in the decade.

Iceland previously abandoned EU negotiations in 2013 after a change in government, but renewed public debate has emerged due to rising living costs and geopolitical shifts following the war in Ukraine.

Under the plan, a “yes” vote in August would allow accession talks to resume, while a “no” vote would end the process entirely.

Lawmakers in the 63-seat parliament voted 34 in favour of the referendum, with eight against and several abstentions or absences.

Officials said the process is intended to give voters more clarity before any final decision on membership is made, given long-standing divisions in public opinion.

Icelandic leaders have previously suggested that fisheries and agriculture would be among the most complex issues in any potential negotiations with the EU.
